Analyzing Indicators of Heavy Traffic¶

This project is about analyzing I-94 Interstate highway data and better understanding strong indicators of traffic for this highway such as weather and time of day.

This dataset was made available by John Hogue, and is found at [UCI Machine Learning Repository].(https://archive.ics.uci.edu/ml/datasets/Metro+Interstate+Traffic+Volume). This dataset contains westbound traffic data from Saint Paul to Minneapolis.

From analyzing the histogram, we can see that the graph is bi-modal.

There is a high frequency of moments with few cars on the road (<1k) and a high frequency of many cars on the road (~5k). The high frequency of moments with few cars on the road could represent the late night/early morning where many are asleep. The high frequency of 5k+ cars on the road could represent rush hour where many people are going to or leaving work.

In order to further analyze this, we will split our dataset into daytime driving (7 am to 7pm) and nighttime driving (7pm to 7am).

From looking at the histograms above and the traffic volume statistics, we can see that daytime traffic volume is slightly left skew and nighttime traffic volume is right skew.

What this means is that most of the instances of high traffic count happen during the day and most of the instances of low traffic count happen during the night.

For us to focus on instances of high traffic, it makes sense to exclude nighttime data in our analysis.

From the graph above, we can see that traffic throughout the year stays approximately uniform except for a dip in July and during late Autumn/early Winter (Holiday season). This makes sense as the holiday season is a common time to see family and people are more likely to be out of the area or driving in a different direction to see family from their usual route on I-94.

From plotting traffic by day of week, we can see that there is a slight increase in average traffic from Monday (day 0) to Friday, but a steep drop off after Friday. This makes sense because Monday through Friday, people are predictably traveling to and from work.

Let's split our data between weekdays and weekends and see if traffic volume trends differ between theses 2 groups.

In [12]:
traffic['hour'] = traffic['date_time'].dt.hour
workdays = traffic.copy()[traffic['day'] <= 4] #0 is Monday, 6 is Sunday
weekends = traffic.copy()[traffic['day'] > 4]
workdays = workdays.groupby('hour').mean()['traffic_volume']
weekends = weekends.groupby('hour').mean()['traffic_volume']

Overall, business days have higher traffic than weekends, but the hours of peak traffic for each group is different. Business days see peak traffic (relative to other business day hours) at 7am and 4-5pm (common rush hours) while during the weekend, we see a smoother trend with peak hours (relative to other weekend hours) from noon to 4pm.

To summarize, we see the highest traffic during work days around the common rush hours of 7am and 4-5pm. Traffic is relatively low at night and on the weekends.
